\title{Emergent Geometry from Entanglement: A Numerical Demonstration Using Heisenberg Spin Chains}

\author{Paul Jarvis}
\affiliation{Independent Researcher, United Kingdom}

\date{June 21, 2026}

\begin{abstract}
We present a fully numerical and model-independent demonstration that smooth
geometric structure can arise directly from the entanglement pattern of a pure
quantum state. Using exact diagonalization of the isotropic antiferromagnetic
Heisenberg spin chain, we compute the ground-state mutual-information matrix
and construct from it a graph Laplacian. A broad suite of diagnostics—
including AICc model selection, extended finite-size scaling, bootstrap
analysis, boundary-slope measurements, synthetic Laplacian benchmarking, and
mutual-information decay exponents—shows that the resulting operator exhibits
the characteristic features of a one-dimensional Laplace--Beltrami operator
with Neumann boundary conditions.

A key quantitative result is the strong agreement between the measured
mutual-information decay exponent and the Luttinger-liquid prediction across
the XXZ critical line. The lowest Laplacian eigenvalue $\lambda_1$ displays a
nonzero finite-size intercept under open boundary conditions (OBC) that
persists, and grows, under periodic boundary conditions (PBC), ruling out a
simple boundary-artefact origin. However, we show that this intercept is not
a fixed physical scale: its magnitude varies by an order of magnitude under
different (equally natural) choices of adjacency normalization, and its value
is substantially reduced---or reversed in sign under PBC---once finite-size
models incorporating the logarithmic corrections expected at a critical point
are included and compared via AICc. We report these sensitivities explicitly
rather than treating the naive $a/N^2+b$ fit as final, and argue that the
existence of a nonzero intercept is on reasonably firm footing while its
interpretation as a ``genuine bulk mass scale'' requires larger system sizes
(e.g.\ via DMRG) to settle. The numerical pipeline developed here, including
these robustness diagnostics, provides a concrete validation tool for
information-theoretic approaches to emergent geometry.
\end{abstract}

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Introduction}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

A central theme in modern quantum gravity is that spacetime geometry may not be
fundamental, but instead emerges from structured patterns of quantum
entanglement. This idea appears in holographic dualities, tensor-network
constructions, and quantum error-correcting codes, yet explicit numerical
demonstrations in microscopic many-body systems remain limited.

In this work we treat the ground state of a simple quantum many-body system—the
isotropic antiferromagnetic Heisenberg spin chain—as a relational dataset. From
its mutual-information structure alone, we construct a graph Laplacian and
examine its spectral properties. Our goal is to determine whether this operator
behaves like a continuum Laplace--Beltrami operator on a one-dimensional
manifold with Neumann boundary conditions, and how this behaviour evolves
between critical and gapped phases.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Methods}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

\subsection{Hamiltonian and ground state}

We study the XXZ Hamiltonian
\begin{equation}
H = J \sum_{i=1}^{N-1}
\left(
S_i^x S_{i+1}^x +
S_i^y S_{i+1}^y +
\Delta S_i^z S_{i+1}^z
\right),
\end{equation}
with $J>0$ and anisotropy $\Delta$. Exact diagonalization yields the ground
state $\lvert \psi_0\rangle$ for system sizes up to $N=18$ in the critical
regime $(-1<\Delta\leq 1)$ and for selected gapped points $\Delta>1$.

\subsection{Mutual-information matrix}

For each site $i$ we compute the reduced density matrix
$\rho_i = \mathrm{Tr}_{\bar{i}} \lvert \psi_0\rangle\langle\psi_0\rvert$, and for
each pair $(i,j)$ we compute
$\rho_{ij} = \mathrm{Tr}_{\overline{ij}} \lvert \psi_0\rangle\langle\psi_0\rvert$.
The mutual information is then
\begin{equation}
I(i,j) = S(\rho_i) + S(\rho_j) - S(\rho_{ij}).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Graph Laplacian construction}

We define a normalized adjacency matrix
\begin{equation}
A_{ij} = \frac{I(i,j)}{\max_{k,\ell} I(k,\ell)},\qquad A_{ii}=0,
\end{equation}
and construct the graph Laplacian $L = D - A$ with $D_{ii}=\sum_j A_{ij}$.

\section{Results}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

\subsection{Zero mode and Neumann boundary conditions}

The lowest eigenvector of $L$ is constant to machine precision, with
$\mathrm{std}(v_0)/|\mathrm{mean}(v_0)|\sim 10^{-14}$ for $N=12$. The first few
nonzero modes exhibit strongly suppressed boundary slopes relative to bulk
gradients, consistent with Neumann boundary conditions.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{test1_zero_mode_boundary_open.png}
\caption{Eigenmodes of the information-derived Laplacian for $N=12$ at
$\Delta=1$ (open boundaries).}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Dispersion and model selection}

For $N=12$, fitting the lowest eight nonzero eigenvalues $\lambda_k$ as a
function of mode index $k$ yields $R^2\approx 0.95$ for both linear and
quadratic fits, but AICc favours the linear model. Pooling data across
$N=6,8,10,12,14,16,18$ also supports a linear trend, though the accessible
sizes do not permit a definitive statement about quadratic dispersion. We
note that a continuum Laplace--Beltrami operator with Neumann boundary
conditions has $\lambda_k\propto k^2$ at low $k$, so a linear-favoured fit is
in tension with the Laplace--Beltrami identification we explore below; we
flag this explicitly rather than treating the two results as consistent, and
return to it in the Discussion.

\subsection{Finite-size scaling of $\lambda_1$ under OBC}

Using the extended set $N=6,8,10,12,14,16,18$, a linear fit
\begin{equation}
\lambda_1(N) = a/N^2 + b
\end{equation}
gives
\begin{equation}
\lambda_1(N) = (8.87\pm0.83)\frac{1}{N^2} + (0.1454\pm0.0109),
\end{equation}
i.e.\ a nonzero intercept at the level of the fit uncertainty (naively
$13.3\sigma$; see Sec.~\ref{sec:robustness} for why this significance should
not be taken at face value).

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{test3_finite_size_scaling_open.png}
\caption{Finite-size scaling of $\lambda_1$ across $N=6$–$18$ (open
boundaries).}
\end{figure}

A resampling of the seven system sizes with replacement (``bootstrap over
$N$'') yields $b=0.1396\pm0.0148$. We caution that, because the underlying
$\lambda_1(N)$ values are deterministic outputs of exact diagonalization
rather than independent noisy measurements, this procedure quantifies
sensitivity to individual system sizes rather than a genuine sampling
uncertainty, and the resulting ``$9.4\sigma$'' should be read in that light
(Sec.~\ref{sec:robustness}).

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{test3c_bootstrap_intercept_open.png}
\caption{Bootstrap distribution of the $\lambda_1$ intercept (open
boundaries).}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Synthetic Neumann benchmark (OBC)}

To assess whether the information-derived Laplacian reproduces the expected
continuum behaviour under open boundaries, we compare its spectrum to that of
an ideal discrete Neumann Laplacian on a chain of the same length. The lowest
modes show close agreement, while higher modes deviate due to short-distance
renormalization effects.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{test8_synthetic_neumann_spectrum_open.png}
\caption{Spectrum comparison between the MI-derived Laplacian and an ideal
discrete Neumann Laplacian for $N=12$ (open boundaries).}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Mutual-information decay exponent vs Luttinger theory (OBC)}

We extract the mutual-information decay exponent along the XXZ critical line
and compare it to the Luttinger-liquid prediction. The measured exponents track
the theoretical values with high correlation, confirming that the MI-derived
geometry captures the correct continuum scaling structure.

\subsection{Critical vs gapped substrates: localization and collapse (OBC)}

Localization is quantified using the inverse participation ratio (IPR). In the
critical regime the lowest modes remain extended, while in the gapped phase
they become localized, signalling the collapse of geometric structure.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Robustness of the finite-size intercept}
\label{sec:robustness}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

The intercept $b$ reported above is the single most consequential number in
this paper, so we subject it to two additional checks that were not part of
the original pipeline: sensitivity to the adjacency normalization, and
sensitivity to the assumed finite-size functional form.

\emph{Normalization.} The adjacency matrix $A_{ij}=I(i,j)/\max_{k\ell}I(k,\ell)$
is one of several equally defensible choices. Repeating the $a/N^2+b$ fit with
$A_{ij}=I(i,j)/\langle I\rangle$ (mean normalization) and with the raw,
unnormalized $I(i,j)$ gives:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\hline\hline
normalization & max & mean & none \\
\hline
$b_{\mathrm{OBC}}$ & 0.145 & 1.800 & 0.137 \\
$b_{\mathrm{PBC}}$ & 1.170 & 5.917 & 0.494 \\
\hline\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{robustness_normalization_sensitivity_open.png}
\caption{Fitted intercept $b$ under three choices of adjacency
normalization (OBC). The intercept remains positive in every case, but its
magnitude is not normalization-invariant.}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{robustness_normalization_sensitivity_periodic.png}
\caption{Fitted intercept $b$ under three choices of adjacency
normalization (PBC). As under OBC, the intercept stays positive but its
size varies by roughly an order of magnitude.}
\end{figure}

The intercept is positive under every normalization tested, for both boundary
conditions, which argues against it being a pure artefact of the specific
$\max$-normalization choice. However, its \emph{magnitude} changes by roughly
an order of magnitude across normalizations, so quoted values such as
$b_{\mathrm{OBC}}\approx0.145$ should be understood as specific to the
max-normalized construction, not as a normalization-independent physical
constant.

\emph{Finite-size functional form.} The $a/N^2+b$ ansatz omits the
logarithmic finite-size corrections generically expected near a critical
(Luttinger-liquid) point, of the same origin as the $\log N$ term in the
critical entanglement entropy. We compare $a/N^2+b$ against models that
include such a term, using AICc, for the max-normalized data:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\hline\hline
model & AICc (OBC) & AICc (PBC) \\
\hline
$a/N^2+b$ & $-51.7$ & $-24.4$ \\
$a\log N/N^2+b$ & $-58.7$ & $-30.0$ \\
$a/N^2+c\log N/N^2+b$ & $\mathbf{-78.5}$ & $-49.4$ \\
$a\log N/N+b$ & $-67.9$ & $\mathbf{-55.1}$ \\
\hline\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

In both boundary conditions, AICc prefers a log-corrected model over the
plain $a/N^2+b$ fit reported in the previous sections. Under the
AICc-preferred model, the extracted intercept changes substantially: for
OBC, $b$ drops from $0.145$ to $0.075$; for PBC, the preferred model
($a\log N/N+b$) gives $b=-0.375$, i.e.\ the sign of the intercept reverses.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{robustness_log_correction_fits_open.png}
\caption{The plain $a/N^2+b$ fit (dashed) versus a log-corrected fit
(solid) for the same max-normalized OBC data. The extracted intercept $b$
differs substantially between the two models.}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{robustness_log_correction_fits_periodic.png}
\caption{As in the previous figure, but for PBC. Here the AICc-preferred
model (not shown; see table above) is $a\log N/N+b$, under which the
intercept changes sign.}
\end{figure}

We therefore do not treat the $13$--$15\sigma$ figures quoted above as a
reliable measure of the significance of a physical mass scale: they measure
the significance of a nonzero intercept \emph{within one particular and, by
AICc, non-preferred finite-size ansatz}. A leave-one-out check (dropping each
$N$ in turn and refitting $a/N^2+b$) shows the intercept moves by only
$\sim$20--25\% of its central value, so the $a/N^2+b$ result is not being
driven by any single system size; the dominant uncertainty is model-form
uncertainty, not sampling noise. Distinguishing between these finite-size
forms with confidence will require larger $N$ than exact diagonalization can
reach, most plausibly via DMRG or MPS methods.

\section{Discussion}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Our numerical pipeline shows that a Laplacian-like operator constructed purely
from ground-state mutual information exhibits several of the defining
features of a continuum Laplace--Beltrami operator on a one-dimensional
interval with Neumann boundary conditions: a constant zero mode, suppressed
boundary slopes in the low-lying modes, and reasonable agreement with an
ideal discrete Neumann spectrum at low mode index. The mutual-information
decay exponent also tracks the Luttinger-liquid prediction closely across the
XXZ critical line under both boundary conditions. We note, however, that
AICc favours a linear rather than quadratic dispersion $\lambda_k(k)$ (Sec.
III.B), which is not what a continuum Laplace--Beltrami operator predicts;
the Laplace--Beltrami identification should therefore be read as a
qualitative, low-mode-index similarity rather than an established
correspondence, pending larger $N$.

The extended-$N$ analysis of $\lambda_1$ reveals a nonzero finite-size
intercept under OBC that does not vanish, and in fact grows, under PBC---this
rules out the intercept being a simple boundary artefact, since a pure
boundary effect would be expected to disappear once the boundary is removed.
This is a genuine and, we think, interesting feature of the MI-derived
Laplacian.

However, as detailed in Sec.~\ref{sec:robustness}, we do not consider the
\emph{size} of the intercept, or its interpretation as a physical mass gap, to
be established. The magnitude of $b$ varies by an order of magnitude across
otherwise-reasonable normalization choices, and an AICc-preferred finite-size
model that includes the logarithmic corrections expected at a critical point
substantially reduces the OBC intercept and reverses the sign of the PBC
intercept. Both of these are choices we made in the original analysis without
testing alternatives, and both turn out to matter more than the
system-size range accessible to exact diagonalization can resolve. We
therefore present the increase from $b_{\mathrm{OBC}}\approx 0.145$ to
$b_{\mathrm{PBC}}\approx 1.17$ (under max-normalization and the $a/N^2+b$
ansatz) as a suggestive but not yet decisive piece of evidence, rather than
as a demonstration of ``a genuinely massive one-dimensional geometry.'' What
the data support more confidently is the qualitative claim that the
intercept is not purely a boundary effect; what they do not yet establish is
its sign-definite existence, size, or physical interpretation once
methodological alternatives are taken into account.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Conclusion}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

We have provided a numerical demonstration that differential-geometric
structure can partially emerge from quantum entanglement alone in a simple
spin chain, together with a broad suite of diagnostics for assessing that
structure. The persistence of a nonzero $\lambda_1$ intercept under both open
and periodic boundaries argues against a purely boundary-artefact
explanation, but our own robustness checks---normalization sensitivity and
AICc-based comparison against log-corrected finite-size models---show that
neither the magnitude nor, under PBC, even the sign of that intercept is
settled at the system sizes accessible to exact diagonalization. We view this
as a limitation to be resolved rather than a result to be asserted: the
pipeline developed here, including the robustness diagnostics of
Sec.~\ref{sec:robustness}, offers a concrete validation tool for
information-theoretic approaches to emergent geometry, and the natural next
step is to extend it to larger $N$ using DMRG or MPS methods, where the
competing finite-size models could be distinguished with confidence. Further
directions include applying the MI-to-Laplacian construction to
higher-dimensional systems and exploring connections to holographic and
tensor-network models of emergent spacetime.

\section{Computational pipeline}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Exact diagonalization of the XXZ Hamiltonian for
$N=6,8,10,12,14,16,18$ at selected $\Delta$.
\item Computation of the mutual-information matrix $I(i,j)$.
\item Construction of the normalized adjacency matrix and graph Laplacian.
\item Eigenvalue decomposition and extraction of eigenmodes.
\item Statistical analysis: AICc model selection, finite-size scaling,
bootstrap analysis, synthetic benchmarking, boundary diagnostics,
Luttinger-parameter sweep, and MI-decay exponent extraction.
\item Robustness diagnostics: repeating the finite-size fit under
alternative adjacency normalizations (mean, unnormalized) and under
finite-size models with logarithmic corrections, compared via AICc
(Sec.~\ref{sec:robustness}).
\end{enumerate}
